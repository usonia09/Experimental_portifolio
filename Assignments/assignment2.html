This appeared unusual as it suggest no condo conversion occured in 2017. It seems to be more plausible that this is a data gap rather than an actual absence of condo conversion. Therefore, the data was filtered to include instances with prior year from 2018 onwards to account for the missing data issue. This approach aims to leverage complete data coverage throughout a contiguous period, 2018-2024. Further data scrutinization once again reveals another data quality issues. The noticeable gap in our data is primarily evident in the information for the year 2021. Unlike our previous approach, we cannot simply eliminate everything before 2021, as doing so would result in the loss of a significant amount of valuable data. To address this issue, we plan to filter out data with incomplete post-property value information selectively during specific analyses that rely on that aspect.

Summary

The investigation into Boston's condo conversion data reveals that while such conversions increase residential space, there is a trade-off with smaller living spaces, fewer amenities, and parking spots. This poses challenges for larger families among the previous residents. Paradoxically, despite these reductions, the overall condition of converted properties tends to improve. The motivation behind condo conversions appears linked to targeting properties in good condition, suggesting investor preferences. However, it is acknowledged that various factors such as geographic location, political atmosphere, and economic considerations also influence conversion attractiveness. A more comprehensive analysis requires additional data to understand these complexities fully.
