Phase One: Sketching Visualizations

Initial Question: What specific trends and shifts in the demographic composition, such as age and gender distribution, can be identified in the United States based on the 1900 and 2000 data? What patterns emerge during the specific periods? How does the demographic landscape in 2000 compare to that of 1900, and are there any notable patterns that require further investigation?

Sketch 1

Design Rationale

In the above sketch I aimed to highlight the noteworthy changes in population distribution particularly the significant increase in the year 2000 compared to the 1900. Notably, the age group of 30-50 exhibts the highest population count in 2000, in stark contrast to 1900, where there was consistent decline in count as age increased. The following rationales elucidate the design choices made to achieve this goal:
  • Utilization of Histograms: The choice of histograms was deliberate, as they excel in capturing the distribution of data.
  • Vertical Stacking the Histograms and having the same axis scalings: The histograms were vertically stacked with the same scaling, not only to emphasize the comparison of distribution between the two years but also to intuitively represent the progression of age.
  • Incorporation of Stacked Bar Format: The histograms were crafted in a stacked bar format to facilitate the depiction of distribution across different genders, adding an extra layer of insight to the visual representation.

While these choices allowed for a comprehensive capture of the overall distribution, it's noted that the gender distribution might not be as emphasized in the graph. Consequently, in the next sketch I aim to delve deeper into the population distribution across age groups, with a specific focus on gender-specific trends.

Sketch 2

Design Rationale

As previously mentioned, the objective of this second sketch is to delve into gender trends across the years 1900 and 2000. To achieve this exploration, the following choices were carefully considered:
  • Selection of the Line Charts: This choice aims to enhance the clarity of trend depiction within and across gender groups. Placing the trends together in a single chart could potentially result in intersecting lines and a more challenging interpretation. The intention is to guide users to focus on one trend at a time—first examining the trend within the male population, then within the female population, and finally across both genders.
  • Utilization of Multi-Line Charts: Line charts were chosen for their effectiveness in facilitating trend comparison across various age groups.

While the multi-line chart design aided in easy dissection, it introduced a potential for misinterpretation, especially if not scrutinized carefully due to the scale breaking required. In the next sketch, I would like to bring more attentional to the total population transformation across age segments.

Sketch 3

Design Rationale

In crafting this sketch, my aim was to visually convey the substantial population increase observed across all age groups. The design choices for this sketch were guided by the following rationales:
  • Aggregation of Consecutive Years: To enhance interpretability and streamline data presentation, I opted to aggregate two consecutive years, shifting from a 5-year to a 10-year range. This adjustment made the visualization less cumbersome while maintaining the integrity of the intended outcome.
  • Utilization of Horizontal Stacked Graphs: The decision to use horizontally stacked bar charts was motivated by the desire to direct focus comparison on population size within each age group across the year 1900 and 2000.

While the horizontal bar charts effectively highlighted the magnitude of the population increase in 2000 compared to 1900, I recognized a limitation. The sketch does not provide insights into gender-specific trends, and the representation inadvertently dilutes the population pattern for 2000. Consequently, it is less evident that the population in their 30s and 40s is the highest for the year 2000, prompting a consideration for potential refinements in future iterations.

Reflection

Each of the three aforementioned sketches possesses its own set of strengths and weaknesses, as briefly highlighted. The first sketch excels in providing a comprehensive overview of the overall distribution but lacks emphasis on gender trends. Conversely, the second sketch adeptly represents gender trends; however, the introduction of a multi-line chart with two axes raises the potential for confusion. Finally, the third sketch effectively showcases the population increase in 2000 compared to 1900 but omits any information about gender dynamics.

In the upcoming phase, my objective is to strike a balance by synthesizing elements from the different graphs, aiming for a more holistic representation of the dataset. I plan to maintain the histogram structure to capture the distribution intricacies, while incorporating the line chart to explore gender patterns. To address the potential confusion introduced by the multi-line chart, I will explore alternative ways of using the line charts. Additionally, I plan to experiment with axis scaling and graph positioning to enhance the visualization and facilitate a clearer depiction of the observed population increase that was previously captured in the third sketch.

Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

The final design seamlessly integrates key elements from previous sketches to present a more comprehensive portrayal of the dataset. Several deliberate design decisions were made to achieve this synthesis:
  • Superposition of Sketch One and Modified Sketch Two: Recognizing the strength of Sketch One in illustrating the general population distribution but its limitation in emphasizing gender trends, an altered version of Sketch Two was incorporated. This modified version effectively captures gender trends, enriching the representation of both aspects of the dataset.
  • Alteration in Sketch Two - Multi-Line Chart Enhancement: Addressing a drawback of the multi-line chart in Sketch Two, where axis breaking posed a potential for confusion, the final design features all lines on the same chart. Annotations indicating gender and year facilitate a clearer representation of gender trends both within and across the years 1900 and 2000.
  • Uniform Scaling for Histograms: The same scaling was applied to the histogram part of the data to reflect the significant increase in population. Notably, the scaling for the line graph wasn't set to the same scale as the histograms, maintaining distinction in trends across the years.
The final design effectively communicates key messages, including the general trend in population distribution, gender-specific distribution, and the population increase between 1900 and 2000 due to uniform axis scaling. While the scaling method highlights population differences, it's acknowledged that a grouped bar chart could have provided clearer visibility of these distinctions. However, software constraints, compounded by the desire to represent gender trends on the same graph, led to the chosen approach in the final design.

Reflection

The sketching phase played a pivotal role in shaping the ultimate design, as the final result is essentially a fusion of refined sketch components. Each sketch addressed specific facets of the dataset, and the synthesis of these key aspects culminated in the comprehensive final design. This amalgamation harnesses the strengths of various elements, such as the clarity provided by histograms in illustrating distribution, the facilitation of trend comparison through line charts, and the strategic manipulation of the axis to incorporate crucial information about population increase—a detail that was evident in the third sketch.
